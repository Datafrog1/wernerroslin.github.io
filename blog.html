üîß What Changed?

  • Unified CSS: Created a single styles.css that handles all global styles and dark mode
  • Shared JavaScript: Added scripts.js for common functionality across all pages
  • Dark Mode Toggle: Every page now has a consistent theme switcher (üîÑ button in top-right)

üéØ Why This Matters

‚öîÔ∏è Battles with AI: The Exponential Complexity of Collaborative Coding

July 11, 2025

Ahoy fellow developers! Today I want to share some hard-won wisdom from the trenches of AI-assisted development. What started as simple projects turned into epic battles against exponential complexity‚Äîand taught me valuable lessons about the limits and strengths of AI collaboration.

üé∞ The Great Roulette Disaster

It began innocently enough: "Let's build a roulette game!" I thought it would be straightforward‚Äîa spinning wheel, some betting logic, maybe some animations. What I didn't anticipate was how quickly things would spiral out of control.

Every feature request seemed simple in isolation:

  • "Add betting on specific numbers"
  • "Include red/black betting"
  • "Add sound effects"
  • "Make the wheel spin realistically"
  • "Add betting history"
  • "Include payout calculations"

But here's the cruel reality: code complexity grows exponentially, not linearly. Each new feature doesn't just add its own complexity‚Äîit interacts with everything that came before. Variables multiply. State management becomes a nightmare. The AI starts suggesting increasingly convoluted solutions to problems that wouldn't exist in a well-architected system.

üß† The Context Window Prison

AI models have a fundamental limitation: context windows. As the codebase grows, the AI loses sight of earlier decisions. It starts treating symptoms instead of causes. What began as elegant code becomes a Frankenstein's monster of patches upon patches.

I found myself in this loop:

  1. Request a feature
  2. AI adds more variables and complexity
  3. Something breaks elsewhere
  4. AI fixes it with even more complexity
  5. Context window fills up
  6. AI loses track of the big picture
  7. Errors start cascading
  8. üîÑ Repeat until madness

üéÆ Tetris: A Different Beast

Abandoning the roulette chaos, I pivoted to Tetris. "How hard could it be?" Famous last words of developers everywhere.

Tetris taught me something crucial about AI collaboration: you need to understand the AI's solution before you can modify it. The game pieces aren't just individual objects‚Äîthey're part of an intricate dance of:

  • Collision detection systems
  • Line clearing algorithms
  • Gravity and timing mechanics
  • Rotation matrices and boundary checking
  • Score calculation and level progression

When I'd ask for "simple" changes like "make the pieces glow," the AI would sometimes rewrite half the rendering system. Not because it wanted to, but because that one visual change rippled through tightly coupled systems.

üóùÔ∏è The Key Insight: Reverse Engineering AI Logic

Here's what I learned: before asking an AI to modify code, spend time understanding how it solved the original problem. This isn't just good practice‚Äîit's survival.

Questions I now ask myself:

  • What patterns is the AI using for state management?
  • How are different systems communicating?
  • What assumptions were made in the original architecture?
  • Where are the tight couplings that might cause cascading changes?

Once I understood the AI's approach, collaboration became exponentially more effective. Instead of fighting against the existing patterns, I could work with them.

ü§ñ The Elephant in the Room: AI Slop

Let's be honest‚Äîthere's still a lot of AI slop out here. Code that works but feels bloated. Solutions that are technically correct but architecturally questionable. Variables named like tempVar2 and functions that do three different things because the AI couldn't decide.

I still encounter this regularly. Sometimes the AI suggests patterns that make me think "this works, but feels wrong." Other times it generates perfectly functional code that just... lacks soul? It's hard to describe, but you know it when you see it.

But hey, whatcha gonna do? ü§∑‚Äç‚ôÇÔ∏è

The way I see it: AI slop is like any other form of technical debt. You can fight it, refactor it, or learn to work around it. The key is recognizing it for what it is and making conscious decisions about when to accept it and when to push back.

Sometimes "good enough" really is good enough‚Äîespecially for experiments, prototypes, or temporary features (like this pirate theme that won't last forever). Other times, you need to draw a line and insist on quality, even if it means explaining to the AI why its first suggestion won't cut it.
